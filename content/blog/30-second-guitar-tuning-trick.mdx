If your guitar sounds off no matter what you do, it's almost certainly this. The fix takes 30 seconds.

I've taught guitar for 15 years. The most common question I get — by a wide margin — is some version of "why does my guitar sound out of tune even after I tune it?"

The answer is almost always the same. And the fix is the same. Here's both.

## Why your guitar goes out of tune

There are three main reasons. In order of likelihood:

### 1. You're tuning each string once and stopping (most common)

When you tighten one string, it changes the tension on the neck. That changes how the other strings sit. So if you tune string 1, then string 2, then string 3 — by the time you're done, string 1 is slightly off again.

This is the "tuning seesaw." The fix is below.

### 2. Your strings are old

New guitar strings stay in tune. Old strings drift constantly because they stretch, collect grime, and lose tension at the tuning posts.

If you can't remember when you last changed your strings, change them. Most players change every 2-3 months of regular play.

### 3. The nut is binding

The nut is the slotted piece of bone or plastic near the headstock. If the slots are too tight, the string catches as you tune and slips back when you let go. You'll tune to perfect pitch, strum, and the open string is sharp.

A drop of pencil lead (graphite) rubbed into each nut slot lubricates it. It's a 30-second fix that lasts a year.

## The 30-second fix

The fix for #1 — the most common problem — is **tune twice, then check the open G**.

Here's the sequence:

1. Tune the low E string to pitch (use a reference — tuner, app, pitch pipe)
2. Tune the A string to pitch
3. Tune the D string to pitch
4. **Stop.** Play the open D string. Listen.
5. If the open D is slightly flat, you've been pushing the neck out of alignment
6. Go back and **re-tune the low E** (it's almost certainly flat now)
7. Then re-tune A, then D
8. **Now check the open G string** — this is the test
9. If G is in tune, you're done
10. If G is slightly off, repeat the cycle one more time — E, A, D, G, B, high E

The whole thing takes 30-60 seconds. After two cycles, most guitars are stable.

## Why this works

You're not actually changing the neck — you're correcting for the small shifts in tension that happen when you add or remove tension from one string. Each cycle, the shifts get smaller. After two or three cycles, you're at equilibrium.

This is exactly how luthiers tune guitars before they ship. It's also how every professional guitar tech tunes your guitar at a concert.

## Tuning by ear (the old-school way)

If you don't have a tuner:

1. Tune the low E string against itself — match the 5th-fret harmonic to the 7th-fret harmonic. When they're both ringing, the string is in tune with itself.
2. Press the low E at the 5th fret — that note is A. Tune the open A string to match.
3. Press A at the 5th fret — that's D. Tune the open D string.
4. Press D at the 5th fret — that's G. Tune the open G string.
5. **Important exception:** Press G at the **4th fret** (not 5th) — that note is B. The major third interval means you can't tune it the same way.
6. Press B at the 5th fret — that's high E. Tune the open high E.

This takes about 2 minutes by ear. It's worth learning.

## The lazy way (what most people do, and why it fails)

Most people:
1. Tune the low E
2. Tune A
3. Tune D
4. Tune G
5. Tune B
6. Tune high E
7. Stop

This is what causes the "out of tune no matter what" feeling. They never check the lower strings after the higher ones pulled the neck slightly.
