I've read 200+ non-fiction books in the last five years. Some I read once and never opened again. Some I read twice. A few I read three times.

Here's the short list of books I keep coming back to — and why they're worth your time.

## The list (in order of how often I re-read)

**1. The Psychology of Money — Morgan Housel**
*Re-read count: 4*
Housel writes 20 short chapters about how we *actually* relate to money — not how we should. The Man in the Car Paradox alone is worth the price of the book.

**2. Four Thousand Weeks — Oliver Burkeman**
*Re-read count: 3*
The best book ever written about time management that isn't really about time management. It's about accepting that you're going to die, and what to do with that knowledge.

**3. Thinking in Systems — Donella Meadows**
*Re-read count: 3*
The clearest primer on systems thinking I've found. If you want to understand why "fixing one thing" usually breaks two others, this is the book.

**4. The Body Keeps the Score — Bessel van der Kolk**
*Re-read count: 2*
If you've ever wondered why your body reacts before your mind can catch up, this explains it. Dense, important, life-changing for some readers.

**5. Atomic Habits — James Clear**
*Re-read count: 3*
Yes, everyone read it. Yes, it's actually good. The 4 Laws framework is the cleanest mental model for habit design I've found.

**6. Range — David Epstein**
*Re-read count: 2*
The case for being a generalist in a specialist world. Comforting for the chronically-curious.

**7. The Beginning of Infinity — David Deutsch**
*Re-read count: 2*
The most ambitious book on this list. Argues that knowledge is the ultimate resource and explains why pessimism is always wrong in the long run.

**8. Debt — David Graeber**
*Re-read count: 1 (but it changed how I think about money)*
5,000 years of debt, told as a single story. The chapter on "The Axe Age" alone justifies reading the whole book.

## What makes a book worth re-reading

The books on this list share one thing: **they rewire how you think, not just what you know.**

A book about productivity tactics (make a to-do list! use the 2-minute rule!) is information. It has a shelf life. A book that changes *how you see* has no shelf life.

When you re-read one of these books two years later, you don't find the same book. You find a new one — because *you've* changed.

## The honest list

The other 192 books I read? Most were fine. Some were great. The ones above are the ones that *stayed*. The 4% of books that did 80% of the work on my thinking.

If you read one of them this month, make it *Four Thousand Weeks*. If you read one this year, make it *The Psychology of Money*.
